## Why Filters Exist

<Callout type="info">
This guide is educational content explaining how our scoring engine works. It is not investment advice. Always do your own research before making investment decisions.
</Callout>

The elimination filters implement a **fail-fast principle**: remove clearly distressed or manipulated companies before the engine spends resources on expensive scoring calculations. Think of them as a quality gate -- if a company cannot clear these basic checks, no amount of scoring nuance will make it a sound investment candidate.

There are six elimination filters in total. All six run regardless of whether an earlier filter has already failed. This means you always get **complete diagnostics** showing every reason a stock was eliminated, not just the first one encountered.

A stock must **pass all six filters** to proceed to dual-track scoring.

## The Six Elimination Filters

### Beneish M-Score

The Beneish M-Score detects earnings manipulation using a composite of financial ratios. It examines patterns across revenue recognition, asset quality, margin changes, and accruals to flag companies whose reported earnings may not reflect economic reality.

- **Threshold**: A score greater than **-1.78** flags manipulation risk and **fails** the filter
- Scores at or below -1.78 are considered safe and pass the filter

<Callout type="tip">
The M-Score was designed to detect Enron-style earnings manipulation. Research has shown it successfully flagged many high-profile fraud cases before they became public.
</Callout>

### Altman Z-Score

The Altman Z-Score is a bankruptcy distress indicator that combines five financial ratios spanning profitability, leverage, liquidity, solvency, and activity into a single composite score.

- **Threshold**: A score below **1.1** indicates severe financial distress and **fails** the filter
- Scores at or above 1.1 pass the filter

<Callout type="warning">
The Utilities sector is exempt from this filter due to their unique capital structure. High leverage is normal and expected for regulated utilities, so applying a standard Z-Score threshold would incorrectly eliminate healthy companies.
</Callout>

### Current Ratio

The current ratio measures short-term solvency by comparing what a company owns in the near term against what it owes.

<Formula>Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities</Formula>

The default threshold is **0.8**, but sector-specific adjustments apply:

| Sector | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Technology | 0.8 |
| Utilities | 0.6 |
| All others | 0.8 |

Companies below their sector threshold fail the filter.

### Interest Coverage

Interest coverage measures how easily a company can pay interest on its outstanding debt. The engine uses a **3-year median** for stability, smoothing out one-off spikes or dips.

<Formula>Interest Coverage = EBIT / Interest Expense</Formula>

Sector-specific thresholds reflect different capital intensity norms:

| Sector | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Technology | 5.0x |
| Utilities | 1.2x |
| Default | 1.5x |

Companies below their sector threshold fail the filter.

### Free Cash Flow Distress

This filter checks for persistent negative free cash flow, which signals a company that consistently burns more cash than it generates.

- **Requirement**: A company must have **3 or more positive FCF years** in the last 5
- **Minimum FCF margin**: Companies with an FCF margin below **-5%** are flagged
- A demonstrably **positive trend** in FCF can rescue borderline cases where the numbers are close to the threshold

### Liquidity

The liquidity filter has two sub-checks to ensure a stock is tradeable enough for meaningful analysis:

**Market Cap** -- minimum market capitalization requirements vary by sector:

| Sector | Minimum Market Cap |
|--------|-------------------|
| Utilities | $1B |
| Energy | $500M |
| All others | $300M |

**Dollar Volume** -- 60-day average daily dollar volume, tiered by market cap size:

| Market Cap Tier | Min Daily Dollar Volume |
|----------------|------------------------|
| Mega Cap | $50M |
| Large Cap | $20M |
| Mid Cap | $5M |
| Small Cap | $2M |

A stock must pass both sub-checks to clear the liquidity filter.

## How Filters Run

All six filters execute regardless of earlier failures. This is a deliberate design choice that provides **complete diagnostics** -- you can see every reason a stock was eliminated, not just the first failure encountered. This makes it much easier to understand why a stock did not qualify and whether the issues are temporary or structural.

A stock must pass **every single filter** to proceed to the scoring phase. There are no partial passes or overrides.

## Common Pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
"Why was my stock eliminated?" -- Check the filter results in the detail panel to see which specific filter or filters failed. Often it is a combination of multiple filters, not just one.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
Sector-specific thresholds exist for a reason. A Utility company with a 0.7 current ratio is healthy by industry standards, while the same ratio would be concerning for a Tech company. Always consider sector context when reviewing filter results.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
Filters are intentionally conservative. A stock that barely fails is still at risk -- the thresholds represent minimum acceptable quality, not aggressive cutoffs. Borderline results should prompt further investigation, not frustration with the filter.
</Callout>

## What Happens Next

Stocks that pass all six elimination filters proceed to **dual-track scoring**, where they are evaluated as both Compounder candidates (steady growth compounding) and Mispricing candidates (undervalued relative to intrinsic worth).
