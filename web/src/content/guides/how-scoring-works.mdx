## Overview

<Callout type="info">
This guide is educational content explaining how Margin's scoring engine operates. Nothing here constitutes investment advice or a recommendation to buy or sell any security.
</Callout>

Every stock that enters the Margin pipeline passes through a four-stage evaluation process:

1. **Elimination Filters** — Binary pass/fail checks that remove distressed, illiquid, or manipulated companies before any scoring begins.
2. **Dual-Track Scoring** — Surviving candidates are independently evaluated on two tracks: Compounder (quality of business) and Mispricing (dislocation from intrinsic value).
3. **Conviction Determination** — Gate-based logic converts continuous scores into discrete conviction levels (EXCEPTIONAL, HIGH, WATCHLIST, or NONE).
4. **Position Sizing** — Conviction level and portfolio context determine a suggested allocation weight.

This guide focuses on stages two and three. For details on elimination filters, see the Elimination Filters guide. For position sizing logic, see the Position Sizing guide.

## Elimination Filters

Before any scoring takes place, every candidate must clear six binary pass/fail checks. These filters exist to remove companies that are financially distressed, too small to analyze reliably, or exhibiting signs of manipulation.

All six filters must pass. A single failure removes the stock from further consideration, regardless of how strong its other characteristics might be. This fail-fast design keeps the scoring engine focused on investable candidates and prevents garbage-in, garbage-out problems downstream.

The Elimination Filters guide covers each filter in detail, including the specific thresholds and the rationale behind them.

## Dual-Track Scoring

Once a stock clears elimination filters, it enters the scoring engine. Here, every candidate is evaluated independently on two parallel tracks:

- **Track A: Compounder** — Tests whether a company compounds shareholder wealth through high-quality operations, durable competitive advantages, and disciplined capital allocation.
- **Track B: Mispricing** — Tests whether a stock is temporarily dislocated from its intrinsic value, offering asymmetric upside with downside protection.

These tracks operate independently. A stock can qualify on Track A alone (a great business at a fair price), Track B alone (an undervalued opportunity with a catalyst), both tracks simultaneously (the strongest signal), or neither. The two tracks capture fundamentally different investment theses, and the engine does not force a stock into one lens when the other fits better.

## Track A: The Compounder

Track A identifies businesses that generate long-term wealth through operational excellence. It uses four sequential gates. All four must pass for the stock to achieve HIGH conviction on this track.

### Moat Evidence

The first gate looks for durable competitive advantages. The engine scans for four moat signatures:

- **Scale Economics** — Unit costs decline as volume grows, creating a structural cost advantage over smaller competitors.
- **Pricing Power** — The company can raise prices without proportional customer loss, indicating brand strength or product differentiation.
- **Switching Costs** — Customers face meaningful friction if they move to a competitor, whether through integration depth, data lock-in, or retraining costs.
- **Capital Efficiency** — The business generates high returns on invested capital relative to its asset base, suggesting an economic moat that protects margins.

The moat durability score is a count from 0 to 4 representing how many of these signatures are detected. The gate requires a moat durability score of at least 2, meaning the company must exhibit at least two distinct moat signatures to proceed.

### Reinvestment Engine

The second gate measures the company's ability to compound capital at attractive rates. It uses a single composite metric:

<Formula>Compounding Power = Incremental ROIC x Reinvestment Rate x (1 - ROIC CV)</Formula>

Each term captures a different dimension of compounding quality:

- **Incremental ROIC** — The return earned on each new dollar of capital invested. Unlike average ROIC, this measures the marginal productivity of recent investments.
- **Reinvestment Rate** — The fraction of earnings the company plows back into the business rather than distributing to shareholders. Higher reinvestment at high ROIC means faster compounding.
- **ROIC CV (coefficient of variation)** — Measures how volatile returns on capital have been over time. The term (1 - ROIC CV) penalizes companies with erratic returns, rewarding consistency.

The gate requires compounding power greater than 0.04. This threshold filters out companies that may have high returns on legacy capital but are not actively reinvesting at attractive rates.

### Capital Allocation

The third gate evaluates management's stewardship of shareholder capital through six sub-factors:

- **Debt Discipline** — Is leverage managed prudently relative to earnings power and industry norms?
- **Organic Reinvestment** — Does the company prioritize internal growth opportunities over financial engineering?
- **Buyback Effectiveness** — When the company repurchases shares, does it do so at prices below intrinsic value?
- **Insider Ownership** — Do executives and directors have meaningful personal wealth tied to the stock?
- **SBC Dilution Tax** — How much shareholder value is eroded by stock-based compensation relative to revenue?
- **M&A Discipline** — Has the company's acquisition history created or destroyed value?

Each sub-factor is normalized to a 0-to-1 scale, then combined via a weighted average into a single capital allocation composite score. The gate requires a composite score above 0.5, indicating that management allocates capital more effectively than the median company in the universe.

### Valuation (Growth Gap)

The fourth gate ensures the stock is not dangerously overpriced, even if the business is exceptional. It uses a Reverse DCF approach: rather than projecting cash flows forward to estimate intrinsic value, it works backward from the current stock price to determine what growth rate the market is implicitly expecting.

That implied growth rate is then compared to the company's demonstrated growth trajectory. The difference is the growth gap. A positive growth gap means the market is pricing in less growth than the company has historically delivered, providing a margin of safety on growth assumptions.

The gate requires a growth gap greater than 0.0, with adjustments based on the current market regime. In elevated-valuation environments, the threshold tightens to demand a larger gap.

## Track B: The Mispricing

Track B identifies stocks where the market price has temporarily disconnected from intrinsic value. Like Track A, it uses four gates that all must pass for HIGH conviction.

### Ensemble Valuation

The first gate establishes whether the stock is genuinely undervalued by aggregating four independent valuation methods:

- **Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)** — Projects future free cash flows and discounts them to present value.
- **Owner Earnings Intrinsic Value** — Uses Buffett-style owner earnings (net income plus depreciation minus maintenance capex) to estimate value.
- **Asset Floor Intrinsic Value** — Calculates a liquidation-based floor value from tangible assets.
- **Peer Comparison Intrinsic Value** — Benchmarks valuation multiples against sector peers to detect relative mispricing.

Beyond the individual estimates, the engine checks for convergence: at least three of the four methods must agree within plus or minus 30% of the median estimate. This convergence requirement prevents the engine from acting on a single outlier valuation model.

The gate requires the current price to be below 60% of the ensemble intrinsic value. In other words, the stock must trade at a 40% or greater discount to the consensus of multiple valuation approaches.

### Downside Protection

The second gate ensures there is a substantial floor beneath the current price. It compares the stock price to the asset floor per share, which represents an estimate of liquidation value.

The gate requires at least a 50% cushion to the asset floor. This means that even in a worst-case scenario where the business is liquidated, the expected loss is limited. Downside protection is what distinguishes a disciplined mispricing bet from speculative bottom-fishing.

### Catalyst

The third gate checks whether there is a near-term reason for the market to close the valuation gap. A cheap stock without a catalyst can stay cheap indefinitely. The engine evaluates three potential catalyst signals and uses the strongest:

- **Insider Buying Percentile** — Are company insiders purchasing shares in the open market? Insider buying is one of the strongest signals of management confidence.
- **Institutional Accumulation Percentile** — Are institutional investors increasing their positions, suggesting smart money is recognizing the opportunity?
- **Earnings Surprise (SUE) Percentile** — Has the company recently beaten earnings expectations, which can trigger analyst upgrades and price re-rating?

The gate requires a catalyst strength above the 60th percentile, meaning the strongest of the three signals must rank in the top 40% of the universe.

### Quality Floor

The fourth gate prevents the engine from recommending deeply discounted companies that are cheap for good reason. It uses return on invested capital as a quality screen:

- **ROIC at or above 8%** — Full pass (score of 1.0). The business earns an adequate return on capital, confirming the mispricing is not driven by fundamental deterioration.
- **ROIC below 8% but improving** — Partial pass (score between 0.5 and 1.0). The business is not yet earning its cost of capital but the trajectory is positive, suggesting a turnaround may be underway.
- **ROIC below 8% and not improving** — Fail (score of 0.0). The business is destroying value and showing no signs of recovery.

## Why Scores Multiply, Not Add

A critical design choice in the scoring engine is that factors within each track are multiplied together, not added:

<Formula>Track A Score = Moat Durability x Compounding Power x Capital Allocation x Growth Gap</Formula>

<Formula>Track B Score = min(Asymmetry, 20) x (Catalyst / 100) x Quality Floor x Convergence Factor</Formula>

In an additive system, a company could score well overall by being exceptional on one dimension and weak on others. A business with a massive moat but terrible capital allocation could still earn a passing grade. Multiplicative scoring eliminates this possibility entirely.

<Callout type="tip">
Multiplicative scoring is a deliberate design choice. If any single factor scores zero, the entire track score becomes zero. This guarantees that a qualifying stock must demonstrate strength across every dimension simultaneously. There is no way to compensate for a fundamental weakness in one area with excellence in another.
</Callout>

This mirrors how compounding works in reality. A company with a strong moat, high reinvestment rates, disciplined management, and a reasonable valuation will compound wealth. Remove any one of those ingredients and the compounding thesis breaks down.

## Conviction Levels

Continuous track scores are converted into discrete conviction levels through a gating system. Each level requires progressively stricter thresholds:

| Level | Track A Requirements | Track B Requirements |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| EXCEPTIONAL | 4/4 gates + compounding > 0.15 + moat >= 3 + gap > 0.08 | 4/4 gates + asymmetry > 5.0 + catalyst > 80% + 4 converging |
| HIGH | 4/4 gates + compounding > 0.08 + moat >= 2 + gap > 0.03 | 4/4 gates + asymmetry > 3.0 + catalyst > 60% + 3 converging |
| WATCHLIST | 3/4 gates + compounding > 0.04 | 3/4 gates + asymmetry > 1.5 |
| NONE | Anything else | Anything else |

A few things to note about how conviction works:

- **EXCEPTIONAL** is deliberately rare. It requires elite performance on every metric, not just clearing the gates. A company must have three or more moat signatures, a compounding power above 0.15, and a growth gap above 0.08 on Track A. On Track B, it requires extreme asymmetry (5x or greater upside), a top-quintile catalyst, and all four valuation methods converging.
- **WATCHLIST** requires only three of four gates, meaning one dimension can be weak. These stocks are worth monitoring but do not meet the bar for a position.
- When both tracks qualify, the engine takes the strongest conviction level from either track and labels the opportunity type as "both." This means a stock that earns HIGH on Track A and WATCHLIST on Track B will be classified as HIGH conviction with opportunity type "both."

## Worked Example

<Example title="TechCo Industries">
TechCo Industries is a fictional enterprise software company with a $15 billion market cap. Here is how it flows through the scoring engine.

**Elimination Filters:** TechCo passes all six filters. It has adequate market cap, liquidity, and financial health. No red flags.

**Track A (Compounder):**

- **Moat Evidence:** The engine detects three moat signatures — scale economics (cloud infrastructure costs decline with volume), pricing power (annual price increases with low churn), and switching costs (deep integration into customer workflows). Moat durability score = 3. Gate passes (threshold: >= 2).
- **Reinvestment Engine:** Incremental ROIC = 22%, Reinvestment Rate = 65%, ROIC CV = 0.15. Compounding Power = 0.22 x 0.65 x (1 - 0.15) = 0.12. Gate passes (threshold: > 0.04).
- **Capital Allocation:** Low leverage, strong organic reinvestment, insider ownership at 8% of shares outstanding, moderate SBC, no major acquisitions. Composite score = 0.65. Gate passes (threshold: > 0.5).
- **Valuation (Growth Gap):** Reverse DCF implies the market expects 12% annual growth. TechCo has delivered 17% annually over the past five years. Growth gap = 0.05. Gate passes (threshold: > 0.0).

All four gates pass. Compounding power of 0.12 exceeds 0.08, moat score of 3 exceeds 2, and growth gap of 0.05 exceeds 0.03. TechCo qualifies for **HIGH conviction on Track A**.

**Track B (Mispricing):**

- **Ensemble Valuation:** The four valuation methods produce a median intrinsic value estimate of $95 per share. TechCo currently trades at $80. That is a 16% discount, but the gate requires a 40% or greater discount (price below 60% of ensemble IV). Gate fails.

Because the first gate fails, Track B evaluation stops. TechCo is not mispriced by the engine's standards.

**Result:** TechCo Industries is classified as a **Compounder** with **HIGH conviction** and a suggested position size of 8%.
</Example>

## Common Pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
**Confusing track scores with conviction levels.** Track scores are continuous values produced by multiplying factors together. Conviction levels are discrete categories determined by gate thresholds. A stock can have a moderately high track score but still land at WATCHLIST or NONE if it fails to clear a specific gate. Always look at the conviction level, not the raw score, when evaluating a candidate.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Assuming WATCHLIST means "buy soon."** WATCHLIST conviction means the stock cleared three of four gates on at least one track but has a meaningful gap in one dimension. It is a monitoring designation, not an actionable signal. WATCHLIST stocks may never reach HIGH conviction if the missing gate represents a structural weakness rather than a temporary shortfall.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Ignoring which track qualified.** A Compounder and a Mispricing opportunity represent fundamentally different investment theses. Compounders are high-quality businesses expected to grow steadily over long holding periods. Mispricing opportunities are undervalued stocks expected to re-rate over shorter timeframes as catalysts materialize. The risk profile, expected holding period, and monitoring criteria differ significantly between the two. Always check the opportunity type alongside the conviction level.
</Callout>
